Telemedicine

Practical Issues

There are many potential problems with this technology that have had little or no impact on conventional surgery. Here are just a few considerations:

  • Software

    The software is highly complex as there any many mechanical, electrical, computational and even communication components to interface. This hinders the surgeon’s demand for user-friendly, intuitive control of the system. The days when a surgeon can control the system abstractly without supervision by an expert with knowledge of the internal system aren’t quite reality.


  • The surgeon

    A fully qualified, experienced surgeon is likely to encounter difficulty in using a telesurgical system simply because his or her ‘picture’ of the operation is much more restricted. Feedback is no replacement for physical presence.


  • Time delays

    If the surgery is being carried out at a different site to the controller, the time delay in the digital signals reaching either station cannot be overlooked. A surgeon may start cutting before realizing that he or she is grasping the wrong tissue causing unnecessary damage. The surgeon must carry out a procedure at a much slower and deliberate pace.


  • Reliability assessment

    It is difficult using mathematical techniques to calculate the mean time between faults in any part of the system. Engineers rely on this in their equipment specification. Again, this is due to the system complexity already mentioned.


  • Fault tolerance

    A fact of life in any software or hardware is bug or fault which the system must be able to deal with. Obvious faults can be trapped and corrected but those which occur under rare conditions are difficult to identify.


  • Security

    ‘Where there is technology there is trouble’ seems to be a recurring theme in the 21st century. Personal data stored and shared with 3rd parties violates the privacy of the public. More worryingly, hackers could be capable of interfering with an operation. Confidence in conventional surgery cannot be matched.


  • Maintainability

    Nothing mechanical is immune to deterioration and so maintaining (e.g. sterilizing, replacing) equipment is a much larger task than ever before. This could prove tricky if part of a system is in a remote area of the world (which is surely one of the motives of telesurgery).


  • Consistency

    Surgeons must have total confidence that a system will perform consistently in different procedures, which is easier demanded than achieved.
