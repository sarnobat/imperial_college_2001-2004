Telemedicine

The Home Asthma Telemonitoring (HAT) System

Why it was developed

Conventional asthma management consists of a patient regularly checking their peak expiratory flow (PEF) which is expressed as a percentage, using medication, and following an action plan suggested by his or her physician (simply a set of lifestyle guidelines) based on the PEF value.

This has worked well for many years. However, recent studies showed that patients did not follow their actions plans closely, frequently didn’t take their medication and reported inaccurate PEF values to their physicians.

The HAT system establishes a long term link between the patient and physician.

What the system consists of

The system is made of three components - a patient unit, a clinician station and a decision support server.

The patient unit is a palmtop with an electronic flowmeter (similar to that conventionally used) connected to it.
The palmtop stores the last 4 months of data locally for the patient’s immediate access. The full data set is stored on a database in the information server having been sent from the palmtop via a secure web interface.
The palmtop checks whether the new data is consistent with that recorded previously, before the decision support server carries out any analysis of its own.

The decision support server, in addition to interpreting changes in the data, ensures that the patient is carrying out the test sessions at the required times. It sends alert messages to the medical staff if the patient doesn’t carry out the test session on time, or doesn’t stick to the suggested medication doses or action plan (though it relies on what the patient has told it).

The staff at the monitoring center check for alerts daily on their workstations with special monitoring software. They can then contact the patients and take appropriate action.
If these alerts are generated outside of working hours then the system automatically calls the nurse’s home telephone.
The nurse may access the data through any web browser.

What a typical patient monitoring session involves

The patient goes through the familiar procedure of using the flowmeter, after being asked to answer some routine questions.

The palmtop analyses both these data, and alerts the medical staff as well as the patient himself/herself if the data gives cause for concern.

Then the palmtop generates a simple qualitative summary of the latest test results and finally, as an extra, gives the patient a ‘tip of the day’ and asks a few multiple choice questions about asthma to improve the patients knowledge of the disease as it may be invaluable some day.

The program is based on Social Cognitive Theory, which (among many other things) anticipates the patient’s mood and responds in suitable ways. It attempts to keep the patient’s self-esteem high by controlled praise, feedback and setting realistic targets.

Data Reporting

There are 3 types of report:

  • Provider
  • Patient
  • Alert

The Provider Report, sent at least once every month to the physician, presents information in numerical table format and flags any data worth bringing to the physician’s attention. It also includes the extent of the patient’s knowledge (based on their performance in the multiple choice questions), and the extent of their obedience to their medication dosage and action plan.

The Patient Report, sent at least once every month to the patient, attempts to cover the same information but in narrative form. It also attempts to convince the patient that a weak adherence to their medication regimen is the cause of their symptoms, should any occur.

The Alert Report is a special version of the Provider Report generated when significantly worse asthma symptoms develop. These reports are treated so seriously that they are automatically faxed directly and immediately to the physician (rather than the nurse) and confirmation of this fax receipt by telephone is mandatory.

Has the system been a success?

Evaluation studies of the HAT system have provided positive results and it seems this is the long term future of managing not only asthma but many other illnesses.
